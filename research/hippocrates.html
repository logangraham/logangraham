Hippocrates: Time-Dynamic Synthetic Controls for Counterfactual Inference (In Progress)

Research under development. Results to be shared January 2018.

In a tweet

I'm building a tool to generate time-dynamic synthetic controls. This exploits information of control units post-treatment to estimate the treatment counterfactual.

Challenge

To do causal inference, you often want to infer a "counterfactual" -- what would have happened to some unit (patient, country, etc.) if they'd received a different treatment. Synthetic controls estimate the counterfactual by learning a mapping from control "donor" units to the treated unit pre-treatment. However, this ignores post-treatment information, such as if donor units become more or less appropriate control group members. It turns out we can leverage post-treatment behaviour of the control units to estimate time-dynamic relationships after treatment, leading to more robust estimates of the counterfactual.

Solution

In the time series case, you can see the counterfactual estimation problem as a combination of a missing data problem and a transfer learning problem. By using multitask Gaussian processes with a specific optimization regime, you can infer the synthetic control more accurately. This enables custom handling of control group surprises using priors over the kernels used.

Results

Forthcoming. First results show (a) robustness to regime changes in donor units and (b) recovery of accurate synthetic controls.
