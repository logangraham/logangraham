#31: All I Want for Christmas is a GPU edition

Hi Oxford AI/ML Community! With Christmas around the corner, surely you don't have anything better to do than spend your break reading more papers?

Interesting News and Links from the past 7 days (papers below)

  • Sexual Harassment in the Statistics & ML Community: this has been a big topic of the last week. First, rumours of questionable events at NIPS, and then an exposé by a statistician of unwanted advances in the Bayesian community. As a result, ISBA and NIPS have both issued statements. This type of news is usually not on this newsletter, hopefully due to its rarity; however, it is important that we as a community reflect deeply on it.
  • A new Andrew Ng startup: Andrew Ng has launched Landing.ai, a new company focusing on tackling problems in manufacturing and production. While automated manufacturing is responsible for the largest chunk of employment shift over the past 50 years, very little is intelligent, and we may be seeing a new "dividing line" in the history of production.
  • Google is back in China: despite a rocky past relationship with the Chinese government, Google will be opening an AI centre in Beijing. Related: China is aiming to create $150 billion (not a typo) in value from AI and be the world leader by 2030. (In 2014 they passed the US in total # of AI/ML papers produced yearly.)
  • Automatic Image Augmentation: a new Python library makes it easy to massively augment your image datasets for vision-based research.
  • Don't understand / don't use Gaussian Processes? A fantastic new tutorial has come out to make them intuitive.

The top 10 papers from the past week

Papers of note: [1] provides a short tutorial on some of the basic mathematics of why deep learning works as well as it does. While we're still short for some of the more important answers, it would be useful to at least understand these results as a baseline.

While [5] was released in June, it develops a new technique that outperforms sequence transduction models – using no recurrence and convolution mechanisms, a simpler architecture, and significantly less training time. Highly recommended.

[6] shows you can predict a deep learning architecture's performance before training it, which could significantly change training practices and thus experimentation time.

[9] is by Oxford researchers within MLRG.

#27: AI is Going to Kill Everyone Edition

Apologies for the grave title. Three very intriguing things have happened in the past few weeks around AI safety, and I recommend you give them a look and think for yourself where you stand. In other news: another TensorFlow release, a new Distill publication, a cookbook with potential, and the Chinese government's AI strategy.


Interesting News and Links

  • "Humankind's Last Invention Ever": three initiatives were launched to promote the development of beneficial AI and prevent the development of severely harmful AI:
    • The Future of Life Institute released Slaughterbots, a short film associated with AutonomousWeapons.org, to reveal how plausible a militarised AI future is. Extremely engaging video – if you like Black Mirror, you'll enjoy this.
    • Canadian researchers called for the Canadian government to join an international ban on the weaponization of artificial intelligence. Canadian/Canadian-related? You can sign the letter here.
    • On November 3rd, researchers launched the Montreal Declaration for a Responsible Development of Artificial Intelligence. You can contribute your thoughts through contributing a brief or answering a survey.
  • Getting deep on the phone: Google released TensorFlow Lite, which can compile pre-trained TensorFlow models for Android and iOS, which should speed-up mobile deep learning applications.
  • Picture books for adults: a great new Distill publication explores how NN's "build up their understanding of images."
  • Cooking with ML: if you follow/know of Ferenc Huszar (his blog is recommended), he's just put out a short note describing to various types of learning barriers, which is short and nice as a refresher.
  • Central planning for AI: the Chinese State Council released "A Next Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan" back in July, which highlights 8 core technologies it aims to build worth the read – see page 9 here.
